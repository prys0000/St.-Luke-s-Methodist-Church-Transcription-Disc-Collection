# Background: St. Luke's and the KTOK Transcription Discs

## The Congregation

Monday, April 22, 1889 marked the Land Run of Oklahoma, in which settlers could claim 160 acres of public land. Six days later — Sunday, April 28, 1889 — a Methodist minister gathered a group in a vacant lot under a shelter named the "Tabernacle Church." Within one month the congregation totaled 501. By 1904 the small brick Tabernacle had become St. Luke's Methodist Episcopal Church, and by 1906 it had grown into a moderately sized building "uptown."

By 1945 the congregation had grown so large that a campaign began to build a sanctuary capable of welcoming all who chose to visit — a great cathedral space with classrooms and activity areas. Groundbreaking took place in March 1948 at 15th and Robinson in downtown Oklahoma City.

## The Recordings

The bulk of the broadcasts from St. Luke's were conducted by **KTOK (1000 AM)**, a radio station serving the Oklahoma City area since 1927. The broadcasts captured the building campaign, sermons, conversations, and music of the era — living history cut directly to disc.

Roughly seventy years later, the **Oklahoma Historical Society, Digitization Division** was contracted to digitize **37 sixteen-inch lacquer discs dating from 1946–1961, along with 3 wire recordings** from the same period.

## Why Do This In-House?

Most cultural heritage institutions face the same pressures around format migration: collections degrade and lose data every day preservation is delayed, while staff respond with "we don't have the resources," "we don't have the skills," or "we cannot possibly handle this media."

The Pryse OHS Method was developed specifically for smaller institutions and those facing budgetary restrictions. Nearly all materials are common household items, with only a couple of specialty pieces (see [`methodology.md`](methodology.md)). The processes were tested and refined on blank or otherwise expendable transcription discs of every base type before being applied to collection materials.

The position taken by this project: "I just don't have time" and "I'm just not trained" are not acceptable statements in the modern archive. Step-by-step guides to cleaning and rehousing most obsolete formats exist — from this project, from The Audio Archive, from the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, and from decades of engineering and conservation literature. Promoting and facilitating the longevity of historical data is the utmost responsibility of the profession. It falls to archivists and curators to innovate new processes, build their own equipment where necessary, involve students in development, and communicate what works back to the field.
